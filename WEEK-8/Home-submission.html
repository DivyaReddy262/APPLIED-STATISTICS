Exercise 1 - ER waiting times

# Given values
lower <- 128
upper <- 147
n <- 64
conf_level <- 0.95

# Step 1: Calculate sample mean
mean_wait <- (lower + upper) / 2
mean_wait
## [1] 137.5
# Step 2: Margin of Error
ME <- (upper - lower) / 2
ME
## [1] 9.5
# Step 3: z* value for 95% confidence
z_star <- qnorm( (1 + conf_level) / 2 )
z_star
## [1] 1.959964
# Step 4: Standard Error
SE <- ME / z_star
SE
## [1] 4.847028
# Step 5: Estimate sample standard deviation
s <- SE * sqrt(n)
s
## [1] 38.77622
# CI from mean and SE
lower_check <- mean_wait - z_star * SE
upper_check <- mean_wait + z_star * SE
c(lower_check, upper_check)
## [1] 128 147

Part - 1

1. This confidence interval is not valid since we do not know if the population distribution of the ER wait times is nearly Normal.

Ans: False

  • The confidence interval is valid because the sample size is large (\(n = 64\)).
  • By the Central Limit Theorem, the sampling distribution of the sample mean is approximately normal even if the population distribution is not.
  • Therefore, the normal model used to construct the interval is appropriate.

2. We are 95% confident that the average waiting time of these 64 emergency room patients is between 128 and 147 minutes.

Ans: False

  • The confidence interval provides information about the population mean, not just the 64 patients in the sample.
  • We are 95% confident that the true average ER waiting time for the entire population of patients lies between 128 and 147 minutes, not just for those in the sample.

3. We are 95% confident that the average waiting time of all patients at this hospital’s emergency room is between 128 and 147 minutes.

Ans: True

  • A 95% confidence interval is used to estimate the population mean.
  • This means we are 95% confident that the true average waiting time for all patients at this hospital’s emergency room lies between 128 and 147 minutes.

4. 95% of random samples have a sample mean between 128 and 147 minutes.

Ans: False

  • The interval (128, 147) was calculated from this specific sample.
  • 95% confidence means that 95% of such intervals constructed from many random samples will contain the true population mean — not that 95% of sample means fall within this particular interval.

5. A 99% confidence interval would be narrower than the 95% confidence interval since we need to be more sure of our estimate.

Ans: False

  • A 99% confidence interval would actually be wider, not narrower.
  • Increasing the confidence level requires a larger margin of error to be more certain that the interval captures the true population mean.

6. The margin of error is 9.5 and the sample mean is 137.5.

Ans:True

  • The margin of error is calculated as half the width of the confidence interval:
    \((147 - 128) / 2 = 9.5\) minutes.
  • The sample mean is the midpoint of the interval:
    \((147 + 128) / 2 = 137.5\) minutes.

Ans: The newspaper claims that the average waiting time exceeds 3 hours (180 minutes). Since 180 minutes is outside the 95% confidence interval of (128, 147) minutes, this claim is not supported by the data. The interval suggests the true mean waiting time is likely lower than 180 minutes.

Task 3: Check Assumptions for the Paired t-Test

  1. Paired Observations:

Ans: In this study, each subject received both Drug 1 and Drug 2, meaning that the two measurements (sleep increase) are taken from the same individual under different conditions. Because the same subjects are used for both treatments, the observations are naturally paired. This pairing controls for individual differences (e.g., baseline sleep levels), making the paired t-test the correct statistical method to use here.

  1. Normality of Differences:
# Load the dataset
data(sleep)

# Create separate vectors for Drug 1 and Drug 2
drug1 <- subset(sleep, group == 1)$extra

Interpretation:

  • Histogram: The distribution is positively skewed (a longer tail on the right), with more values clustered between 0 and 2 hours. This supports the Shapiro–Wilk test result that normality is violated.

  • Q–Q Plot: The points deviate from the red reference line, especially at the upper end, indicating departure from normality.
